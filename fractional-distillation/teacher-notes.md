# Fractional Distillation — Interactive Visualisation

**File:** `index.html` (fully self-contained — no internet needed once the file is open)
**Spec:** AQA GCSE Combined Science: Trilogy, Chemistry Paper 2 topic *Crude oil and fuels* (4.7.1) — works equally as a forward reference during Paper 1 teaching.

## Getting it to students

The file is a single HTML page. Share it through Showbie or Seesaw; students open it in Safari on their iPads. It also works full-screen on the classroom display for whole-class teaching.

## The two modes

**Guided steps** (default) — seven steps you advance with Next/Back. Designed for I Do: each step dims the parts of the diagram that don't matter yet and gives a short caption with Tier 3 vocabulary in bold. The sequence: mixture → heating → vapour enters → temperature gradient → condensing → collecting → the chain-length pattern.

**Watch it work** — the column runs continuously with play/pause and a speed slider. Suits We Do questioning ("where will this long chain condense?") and student exploration during You Do.

## Other features

- **Tap any fraction label** for an info card: carbon chain length, boiling range, use, and bars comparing boiling point, viscosity and flammability.
- **"Why? Chain length"** (top button) — a slider from 1 to 60 carbon atoms showing which fraction a chain belongs to and how its properties change. This is the link between chain length and properties that foundation students find hardest.
- **"Key words"** — a glossary of the Tier 3 vocabulary: hydrocarbon, fraction, evaporate, condense, boiling point, temperature gradient, viscous, flammable.
- The counters next to each label tally how many molecules have been collected — useful for asking why the mix collected at each level differs.

## Suggested lesson fit (MBA Lesson System)

- **Do Now:** retrieval on states of matter / boiling points before opening the tool.
- **I Do:** Guided steps 1–5 on the big screen, narrating each stage.
- **We Do:** switch to Watch mode; cold-call predictions about where molecules will condense and why.
- **You Do:** students open the file on iPads, work through the fraction cards and the Why slider, and record one use + one property for each fraction.
- **End & Send:** "A hydrocarbon has 30 carbon atoms. Where does it leave the column, and why?" — answerable entirely from the tool.
